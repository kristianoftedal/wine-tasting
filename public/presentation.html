Wine terms explained

Words you'll meet when tasting

Wine descriptions use a specific vocabulary. Here is what each term actually means — in plain language.

Aroma
Smell / nose
Everything you can smell before and after lifting the glass to your lips. Common aromas include dark berries, vanilla, pepper, citrus, or flowers. A complex wine has many distinct aromas layered on top of each other.
Body
Fylde · 1–10
How heavy or substantial the wine feels in your mouth. Think of the difference between water (light) and whole milk (full-bodied). A light-bodied wine feels thin and refreshing; a full-bodied wine feels rich and weighty. Rated 1 (very light) to 10 (very full).
Acidity
Friskhet · 1–10
The sharpness or tartness of the wine — the same sensation as lemon juice on your tongue, just much milder. High acidity makes a wine feel lively and crisp; low acidity makes it feel soft and round. Rated 1 (very soft) to 10 (very sharp).
Tannins
Snerphet · 1–10
The dry, slightly grippy sensation you feel on your gums and tongue after swallowing red wine — similar to drinking very strong black tea. Tannins come from grape skins and oak barrels. Only red wines are scored on tannins. Rated 1 (silky smooth) to 10 (very astringent).
Sweetness
Sødme · 1–10
Whether the wine leaves a sugary taste on the palate, from completely bone-dry (like a crisp Chablis) to dessert-sweet (like a Sauternes). Most still red wines are dry or close to it. Rated 1 (bone-dry) to 10 (very sweet).
Finish
Ettersmak
The taste that lingers in your mouth after you swallow. A "long finish" means the flavour stays with you for many seconds — often a sign of a high-quality wine. A short finish disappears almost immediately.
Flavour families
App concept
The app groups every tasting word into one of six broad families: Fruit, Spice, Herbs, Floral, Oak/barrel, Mineral. "Blackcurrant" and "cherry" both belong to the Fruit family, for example. Matching the right family earns partial credit even if the exact word differs.

How scoring works

Comparing your tasting notes

Every wine has an official description from Vinmonopolet. When you write your own notes, the app compares them using three overlapping checks — so you are fairly rewarded even when you use different words for the same thing.

1
Meaning check — what did you mean?
An AI reads both your notes and the official description and judges how similar the overall meaning is. If you wrote "berries and spice" and the wine says "blackcurrant and pepper", it still recognises the connection. This is the foundation of your score.
2
Key-word check — did you name it precisely?
The app looks for matching root words — the base form of a word. "Bær", "bæret", and "bærpreg" all count as the same word. Specific terms like "solbær" (blackcurrant) earn more credit than vague ones like "frukt" (fruit), because naming something precisely shows real tasting skill.
3
Family check — are you in the right neighbourhood?
Even if you miss the exact word, the app checks whether you identified the right flavour family. Writing "cherry" when the wine says "blackcurrant" still earns partial credit — both are in the Fruit family. Writing "herbs" when the wine says "mint" earns full credit because they share the exact same sub-category.
The golden rule
Precise words can only add points — never take them away
The AI meaning score is always your starting point. Layers 2 and 3 can only push it higher. You are never penalised for being imprecise; you are rewarded for being accurate.
Example in practice

The wine's description says "dark berries, blackcurrant, cherry". You write "solbær og kirsebær" (blackcurrant and cherry).

The AI recognises strong meaning overlap → solid base score.
"Solbær" and "kirsebær" are specific fruit words → bonus points added.
Result: a very high tasting-note score.

Why colour scored 100 but taste scored 68

The taster nailed the colour exactly ("dyp rød" matches "dyp rød" — full marks). For aroma, "røde bær, mørk frukt, krydder" hits the same flavour families as "modne røde og mørke bær, krydder" — strong match at 82. For taste, the taster reused the same words they wrote for aroma, while the official taste description emphasises structure, juiciness and length — nuances the taster didn't mention — so the taste score drops to 68.

Personalised picks

Wine recommendations

The app learns what you love from wines you rated 8 or higher, then finds new wines you are likely to enjoy — without you having to do anything.

1
It learns your taste fingerprint
The app looks at body, acidity, tannins, and sweetness across every wine you rated 8 or above and builds a picture of what you consistently enjoy.
2
It finds wines that match your fingerprint
From the full wine catalogue, it selects the 100 wines whose structure is closest to what you already love.
3
It ranks them by flavour similarity
Those 100 candidates are compared to the flavour descriptions of your favourite wines using AI. The ones that sound most like what you enjoy rise to the top.
4
Only wines you haven't tried appear
Wines you've already tasted are always excluded — every recommendation is a genuine new discovery.
